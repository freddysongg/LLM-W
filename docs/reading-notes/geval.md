# G-Eval: NLG Evaluation using GPT-4 with Better Human Alignment

Reading notes on Liu et al. (2023), EMNLP. Full citation and bibliography anchor at [`docs/references.md#r1--g-eval`](../references.md#r1--g-eval).

## Main contribution

The paper's central claim is that a prompt-only evaluator built on GPT-4 with chain-of-thought reasoning and auto-generated evaluation steps outperforms every reference-based n-gram metric (ROUGE, BLEU, METEOR) and every then-current learned metric (BERTScore, MoverScore, UniEval) on correlation with human judgment across three natural-language-generation tasks. On SummEval (news summarization) the paper reports a summary-level Spearman of 0.514 averaged across coherence, consistency, fluency, and relevance; on Topical-Chat (dialogue response generation) it reports 0.481 on the engagingness dimension. The novel mechanism is the "fill-in" template that treats evaluation as a two-stage generation problem: the evaluator LLM first expands a short task description into a numbered rubric of evaluation steps, and only then produces a score against those steps. A secondary contribution is the probability-weighted decoding trick that smooths GPT-4's quantized Likert scores by taking an expectation over the 1-through-5 token logits.

## Method

G-Eval operates in two stages. Stage 1 is the auto chain-of-thought: the prompt contains the task definition and the evaluation criterion (e.g., "coherence: the collective quality of all sentences") and asks the model to emit an ordered list of evaluation steps. Stage 2 is the scoring call: the generated steps are concatenated into a single prompt together with the source document, the candidate output, and an instruction to produce a 1-5 Likert rating. Both stages use GPT-4 (`gpt-4-0314` in the paper) with temperature configurations drawn from the original task's official evaluation template. The headline scoring refinement is probability-weighted decoding: instead of taking argmax over the score-token distribution, the authors compute the expectation sum over p(k) * k for k in {1..5} using the top-k logprobs returned by the API, which yields a continuous score and resolves ties between candidates. Evaluation spans SummEval (1,600 human-rated summaries on four dimensions), Topical-Chat (engagingness and groundedness), and the QAGS consistency benchmark.

## Key limitation

G-Eval is validated exclusively on open-ended NLG tasks with Likert-scale human ratings; the paper shows no evidence that the approach transfers to reasoning, code, tool-use, or adversarial settings, and its "meta-evaluation" collapses when human raters themselves disagree (inter-annotator agreement is not reported cleanly for all dimensions). The 0.514 Spearman headline, while state-of-the-art in 2023, still leaves roughly half the human-judgment variance unexplained, and the paper documents a self-preference bias in which GPT-4 scores GPT-4-generated summaries higher than human-written ones even when humans prefer the latter. Probability-weighted decoding also requires logprobs access, which not all deployments expose. Reproducibility depends on a specific GPT-4 snapshot (`gpt-4-0314`) that OpenAI has since deprecated, and the paper does not report cost or latency. LLM-W's v4 plan explicitly rejects Likert scoring in favor of binary pass/fail (per R3 and R11), so G-Eval's correlation numbers do not transfer to our setting directly.

## Why it applies to LLM-W

We adopt G-Eval's auto-generated-evaluation-steps mechanism in `backend/app/services/eval/geval.py`, specifically in the `GEvalJudge` class and its companion `_EvalSteps` Pydantic response model. Our `_generate_steps_openai` helper issues a dedicated LLM call that expands `rubric.description` plus the list of `Criterion` objects into a structured list of steps, which `_EvalSteps` constrains to between 3 and 7 entries (G-Eval's prompt is unbounded; we bound it to keep the downstream judge prompt compact). The generated steps are cached in `GEvalJudge._steps_cache` keyed on `sha256(rubric.model_dump_json())` so rubric edits invalidate the cache automatically. At scoring time, `evaluate` retrieves the steps, injects them via `_build_messages(..., eval_steps=steps)` from `openai_judge.py`, and delegates to `OpenAIJudge.score_from_messages`. We diverge from G-Eval on two axes: verdicts are binary pass/fail rather than Likert (per R3/R11), and each rubric under `rubrics/` carries its own few-shot examples, so the prompt is always contextualised. `rubrics/faithfulness.yaml` lists `R1` in `research_basis` alongside `R3`.
