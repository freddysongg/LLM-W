# ChainPoll: A High-Efficacy Method for LLM Hallucination Detection

Reading notes on Friel & Sanyal (2023), Galileo AI. Full citation and bibliography anchor at [`docs/references.md#r4--chainpoll`](../references.md#r4--chainpoll). The v4 plan attributed the technique to DataDog; the primary source is the Galileo paper and our bibliography note in `docs/references.md` records the discrepancy.

## Main contribution

The paper's central claim is that a small ensemble of same-model judge calls at nonzero temperature, aggregated by majority vote, produces a hallucination detector whose calibrated accuracy and AUROC materially exceed any single-shot judge prompt on the same underlying model. The authors introduce two variants: ChainPoll-Correctness for open-domain factuality and ChainPoll-Adherence for retrieval-augmented grounding. Alongside the method the paper ships RealHall, a hallucination benchmark aggregating four task settings (open-domain QA, closed-book QA, RAG, and summarization) specifically chosen to span cases where "hallucination" means different things, and reports that ChainPoll on GPT-3.5 matches or beats more expensive baselines like SelfCheckGPT and GPTScore while using fewer total tokens than multi-prompt chain-of-thought approaches. The conceptual contribution is the framing of chain-of-thought as a source of useful disagreement: sampled reasonings diverge precisely where the single-shot prompt is least confident, and majority voting exploits that.

## Method

Given a judge prompt (system instructions, the rubric question, and the candidate response), ChainPoll issues N independent chat-completion calls against the same model at a fixed nonzero temperature. The paper uses N between 3 and 5 and temperatures in the 0.2-0.7 range, with the empirical sweet spot around N=5 at T=0.5 for RealHall tasks; N=3 is reported as a cost-efficient baseline that already captures most of the gain. Each call produces a chain-of-thought followed by a binary verdict token (the prompt enforces a yes/no ending). Verdicts are extracted by regex and reduced by simple majority; the paper also reports the distribution across calls as a calibrated confidence signal (e.g., 5-of-5 vs 3-of-5). All N reasonings are retained for audit purposes. Evaluation is AUROC and accuracy against held-out human-labelled hallucinations on the four RealHall subsets, with cost and latency reported in tokens and wall-clock seconds.

## Key limitation

ChainPoll is a single-model panel: N copies of the same judge share the same training distribution, pretraining biases, and failure modes. If GPT-4 systematically misreads a category of hallucination (e.g., subtle numeric errors in units), the ensemble inherits that blind spot linearly. The multi-model generalisation (cross-family panels like PoLL, Verga et al.) addresses this but the v4 plan explicitly dropped it on cost and complexity grounds, so LLM-W carries the same caveat. Cost scales strictly linearly with N, tripling the API bill at N=3 relative to single-shot; the paper quantifies token cost but does not discuss how the N-vs-accuracy curve behaves past N=5. Temperature greater than zero introduces run-to-run nondeterminism: the same (case, rubric) pair can flip majority verdicts across replays unless a seed is pinned, which complicates regression testing. The RealHall benchmark is also Galileo-authored, so external replication of the specific accuracy numbers is thin.

## Why it applies to LLM-W

We implement ChainPoll as `ChainPollJudge` in `backend/app/services/eval/chainpoll.py`. The class wraps an injected `OpenAIJudge` (the same base judge used by `GEvalJudge`) and delegates verbatim when `rubric.chainpoll` is `None`, so the wrapper imposes zero cost on rubrics that opt out. When the config is present, `_gather_scores` dispatches `config.n` parallel calls via `asyncio.gather` using `OpenAIJudge.score_from_messages` with `temperature=config.temperature` and `judge_model=config.model`. `_majority_verdict` reduces the verdicts with a deterministic tie-break (first call wins; irrelevant at our pinned N=3 because ties are impossible with an odd N and binary verdicts). The aggregated `Score` stores a JSON-serialised payload in `reasoning` containing every call's verdict, reasoning, cost, and latency, with a sha256 hash of that payload as `response_hash` for replay. The sole caller is `rubrics/hallucination.yaml`, which pins `chainpoll: {n: 3, model: gpt-4o-mini-2024-07-18, temperature: 0.3}` and lists `R4` in `research_basis`. Other rubrics use single-call Tier-2 judging to keep the cost multiplier localised to the rubric where the research evidence applies.
