# Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback

Reading notes on Bai et al. (2022), Anthropic. Full citation and bibliography anchor at [`docs/references.md#r6--constitutional-ai`](../references.md#r6--constitutional-ai).

## Main contribution

The paper's central claim is that a harmless, non-evasive assistant can be trained using only AI-generated supervision for harmlessness, with human feedback restricted to helpfulness labels. This matters because human harmlessness labelling is expensive, slow, and exposes annotators to toxic content; replacing it with AI feedback scales cheaply and keeps humans out of the worst inputs. The novel mechanism is the two-stage "constitution" loop: a small written document of principles (examples: "choose the response that is least harmful," "avoid sounding preachy or condescending," "do not provide information that could enable serious harm") drives both supervised self-revision and AI-generated preference labels. Empirically the paper reports that a CAI-trained 52B model is rated by humans as strictly more harmless than an RLHF baseline at matched helpfulness, and that the resulting assistant refuses harmful requests while explaining its reasoning rather than dodging. The paper also introduces "chain-of-thought" preference labelling inside the feedback loop itself.

## Method

Training proceeds in two phases. Phase 1 (Supervised Constitutional AI): starting from a helpful-only RLHF model, the authors sample responses to a bank of red-team prompts, then ask the model itself to critique each response against a randomly sampled constitutional principle and rewrite it to comply. The (prompt, revised-response) pairs become a supervised fine-tuning set; one or more revision rounds are applied before SFT. Phase 2 (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback, RLAIF): the SFT model is prompted to choose between two candidate responses to a harmfulness prompt, conditioned on a constitutional principle; the chosen/rejected pairs train a preference model, which in turn serves as the reward in a standard PPO RL phase. Helpfulness preference pairs come from humans as in vanilla RLHF; harmlessness preference pairs come entirely from the AI feedback loop. The constitution is a few dozen short principles sampled per critique; the authors ablate principle count and find diminishing returns past roughly sixteen.

## Key limitation

The constitution is a proxy for human values, not a grounding: whatever bias, omission, or cultural assumption the principles encode is inherited by the policy, and the paper does not propose a systematic way to audit a constitution for coverage. "Honest" in particular is evaluated only against the model's own distribution of opinions rather than against external facts, so a CAI-trained model can be confidently wrong while satisfying the honesty principle. Results are reported on Anthropic's own 52B model family with Anthropic's specific constitution; cross-lab generalisation is asserted but not demonstrated, and subsequent work has shown that smaller models struggle with the self-critique step because they cannot reliably identify harms in their own outputs. The helpfulness-harmlessness tradeoff is measured on Anthropic's internal red-team prompts, which undersample some real-world harm categories. Finally, CAI does not address emergent capabilities like jailbreak robustness under adversarial prompts, where the safety guarantee degrades substantially.

## Why it applies to LLM-W

We do not train with CAI. We adopt only the HHH dimensional taxonomy (helpful, honest, harmless) as the structure of the Tier-2 safety rubric in `rubrics/safety.yaml`, where each dimension appears as an atomic binary `Criterion` with additive points (`helpful: 1`, `honest: 2`, `harmless: 2`). The rubric's `description` names Constitutional AI directly, and `research_basis: [R6, R12]` pairs CAI with OpenAI Moderation. The runtime flow is two-tier: `tier1.py` runs the `moderation_openai` validator (see `_moderation_openai`) against `omni-moderation-latest` first, and a flagged response short-circuits to `fail` with `reason` listing the flagged categories, never reaching the judge. If moderation passes, the Tier-2 judge (an `OpenAIJudge` scoring against the three HHH criteria with the five few-shot examples in `rubrics/safety.yaml`) produces a binary verdict and a per-criterion breakdown. The division is deliberate: moderation catches clear-cut policy violations cheaply, while the CAI-derived criteria score the grey-zone cases where "harmless" and "honest" require reading context rather than keyword matching.
